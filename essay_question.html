Should page authors use non-standard additions to HTML?

What if they are proposed specs in non-finalized versions?

                    I do not believe that page authors should use non-standard additions in their HTML code. 
                    By doing so, I think it will decrease the compatibility of their pages on different browsers. 
                    Also, the needs of the readers should be more important than a “desired effect,” especially 
                    if that “effect” may impact the accessibility for users with visual or other impairments. I feel 
                    that this would be unethical.
  
                    I also feel that if it is then used by other developers from different companies, the introduction 
                    of non-standardized additions to HTML would set back the years of work that went into creating 
                    standards in the first place. The last thing the internet needs is a rehash of the fight between 
                    Netscape and Microsoft (1). 

                    If the non-standard additions are part of a non-finalized version of the code, it should be 
                    reviewed by the department as whole and the final decision to use the code should come down 
                    to a consensus vote. In the article, “Giving Web Standards a Seat at the Table", the author, 
                    Jay Hoffman , points out that when Molly Holzschlag spoke with the Internet Explorer team 
                    at Microsoft,she was surprised to find that there was some support for standardization among 
                    some of the team members (1). A consensus vote on the use of non-standard additions would 
                    ensure that the entire webpage team would not only be aware of the proposed use of the addition,  
                    but it would also give them a chance to voice any concerns or oppositions they may have over the 
                    use of the code. Instead, the team should work with theW3C to integrate any proposed non-standard 
                    additions into the next batch of updated W3C HTML5 standards.
                
            Reference:
            (1)	 Jay Hoffman. “Giving Web Standards a Seat at the Table.”  History of the Web, 19 Jan. 2022, .
